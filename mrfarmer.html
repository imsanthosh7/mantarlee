Activated Charcoal

The earth's atmosphere contains 750 billion tons of carbon and there's twice as much carbon in the soil, 1500 billion tons of it. For thousands of years human activities like deforestation and cultivation have released 500 billion tons of carbon from the soil into the atmosphere. Agriculture currently generates 19–29% of total greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. Modern conventional, industrial agriculture has accelerated the pace of carbon emissions that contributes to the warming of our planet.

Activated Charcoal used in agriculture can reduce around 30%+ excess of CO2 already in the atmosphere. The charcoal greatly increases soil fertility through its capacity to retain water and nutrients. In some cases, crop yield can increase minimum 25% onwards.

Vermi-compost

Plants absorb carbon di oxide from the air in a process called photosynthesis and thus the carbon turns into sugars and other carbon compounds and are secreted through the roots of the plants.

These compounds serve as food for microorganisms. Microorganisms play foremost role in soil formation and soil ecology because they as 'natural soil engineers' regulate the flux of nutrients to plants and prop up nitrogen fixation, and ultimately promote detoxification of naturally occurring inorganic and organic pollutants in soil.

The little creatures like earthworms also called the macro organism in the soil, through consumption of decaying organic waste, excretion and the act of boring through the soil, work as a glue to keep the soil together and aggregate it.

Bee Keeping

A single bee colony with around 10,000 bees can significantly benefit small farmers. These bees support natural pollination, leading to better crop yields. Additionally, farmers can extract and sell honey, creating a valuable source of supplementary income.

Agro Forestry

Agroforestry is the intentional integration of trees and shrubs into crop and animal farming systems to create environmental, economic, and social benefits. It has been practiced around the world for centuries. Further, if agroforestry is practiced as a plantation of fruit saplings, leading to additional income for farmers, it becomes one of the best climate-smart practices to build farmer resilience during challenging times. Examples include: Balaji Lemon, Tamarind, Chickoo, Guava, Pomegranate, Coconut, Native Badam, and Jamun.
